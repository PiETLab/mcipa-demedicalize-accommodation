
# Toward De-Medicalized and Equitable Academic Accommodation Systems in Postsecondary Education

## Executive Summary

Postsecondary accommodation systems were created to improve access for disabled students. In practice, however, many current models rely heavily on medical documentation, formal diagnosis, and individualized disclosure processes that can create new barriers rather than remove them. Students are often required to secure costly assessments, obtain physician letters, repeatedly verify chronic conditions, and navigate complex administrative procedures before receiving support. These requirements disproportionately burden students navigtating interlocking oppressions, including low-income students, racialized students, international students, students with episodic or mental health disabilities, and students whose disabilities are not easily legible through conventional medical frameworks (Waterfield & Whelan, 2017; Shanouda & Spagnuolo, 2021).

A growing body of scholarship suggests that accommodation systems frequently reproduce existing inequalities in higher education. Research identifies barriers including stigma, fear of discrimination, lack of awareness of services, negative faculty interactions, distrust of institutional processes, and burdensome documentation requirements (Lindsay et al., 2018; Lyman et al., 2016; M. et al., 2010). Other studies show that students from higher socioeconomic backgrounds are more likely to obtain expensive psychoeducational assessments and navigate institutional procedures successfully, while working-class students face structural exclusion (Waterfield & Whelan, 2017). Critical scholarship further argues that race, class, disability, and migration status intersect in ways often ignored by one-size-fits-all accommodation systems (Stapleton & James, 2020; Baker, 2019).

At the same time, many institutions have begun implementing alternative approaches. Universities such as Portland State University, the University of Arizona, Syracuse University, and Cornell University have adopted more flexible documentation standards, case-by-case determinations, common-sense review processes, or explicit commitments to reduce the medicalization of accommodations. These models demonstrate that less burdensome systems are both possible and already operational in peer institutions.

This Advocacy Paper argues that postsecondary institutions should move from a narrow compliance-based accommodation model toward an access-centered access model. The dominant framework often treats access as an individual transaction: a student proves disability, receives a letter, and negotiates accommodations alone. By contrast, an access-centered model understands access as a shared institutional responsibility requiring proactive design, reduced bureaucracy, transparent processes, and recognition of intersecting forms of oppression (Sins Invalid, 2019; Mingus, 2011).

## Core Findings

1.     **Medical documentation requirements function as structural barriers.**  
They impose financial, administrative, and emotional costs on students, particularly those already marginalized.  

2.     **Current systems often privilege students with economic and cultural capital.**  
Students who can afford assessments, understand bureaucratic processes, or advocate confidently are more likely to succeed.  

3.     **Disclosure-based systems create avoidable harm.**  
Many students avoid accommodations because of stigma, fear of retaliation, or concern that they will be perceived as less capable.

4.     **Alternative models already exist.**  
Several universities have adopted flexible, non-medicalized approaches without compromising academic standards.

5.     **Access must move beyond compliance.**  
Institutions should treat accessibility as a campus-wide responsibility rather than the isolated function of a disability office.

## Key Recommendations

1.     Reduce reliance on medical documentation and accept broader forms of evidence, including self-attestation in appropriate cases.

2.     Create case-by-case review systems grounded in access, equity, and good faith.

3.     Introduce transparent appeal and conflict-resolution mechanisms.

4.     Expand faculty responsibility for inclusive pedagogy and access-centered course design.

5.     Collect equity data to monitor racialized, class-based, and disability-based disparities in academic accommodation access.

6.     Advocate provincially for funding models that do not incentivize restrictive gatekeeping.

## Why Action Is Urgent

Institutions increasingly promote diversity, equity, inclusion, and belonging while maintaining accommodation systems that many students experience as adversarial, slow, and exclusionary. This gap between institutional commitments and lived reality undermines student success, retention, and trust. Reforming accommodation systems is therefore not a peripheral administrative matter. It is central to educational equity and the public mission of public post-secondary educational institutions in Canada.

# 1. Introduction

Across Canada and internationally, postsecondary institutions have expanded their public commitments to diversity, equity, inclusion, accessibility, and student wellbeing. Yet many disabled students continue to encounter accommodation systems that are costly, bureaucratic, reactive, and difficult to navigate. Instead of functioning as straightforward pathways to educational participation, these systems often require students to prove eligibility through medical documentation, disclose sensitive personal information, repeatedly renew records, and negotiate support within uneven institutional power relations.

The contradiction is clear: institutions increasingly speak the language of inclusion while preserving access systems that many students experience as exclusionary. This Advocacy Paper addresses that contradiction and proposes a pathway toward de-medicalized, equitable, and institutionally accountable accommodation systems.

The issue is not whether accommodations matter; they do. Research consistently shows that accommodations can be crucial to persistence, degree completion, and educational survival for many disabled students (Baker, 2019). The issue is that the current model frequently places the burden of access on students themselves. Students must identify as disabled in institutionally recognized ways, obtain approved evidence, navigate unfamiliar systems, and advocate repeatedly for needs that institutions could often anticipate and design for in advance.

# 2. The Current Accommodation Model

Most postsecondary accommodation systems follow a common sequence:

1.     Student experiences barriers to access 

2.     Student is compelled to seek diagnosis or medical documentation

3.     Student is compelled to disclose medical documentation, disability, and/or "functional limitations" to accessibility office

4.     Accessibility Office determines eligibility

5.     Accommodation letter issued

6.     Student negotiates implementation with faculty or staff

7.     Student re-engages system on an annual basis 

This model appears neutral and orderly. In practice, each stage creates obstacles.

## Documentation Burdens

Students may need physician letters, psychoeducational testing, psychiatric records, or specialist reports. These documents can be expensive, delayed, geographically inaccessible, or difficult to obtain for students without family doctors or extended health coverage. Students with chronic or lifelong disabilities may be asked to repeatedly re-confirm conditions that are permanent or well-established (Shanouda & Spagnuolo, 2021).

## Disclosure Burdens

Disclosure is often framed as a simple administrative step. Research shows it is frequently a complex social and political decision shaped by stigma, fear of judgment, prior discrimination, and uncertainty about consequences (Lindsay et al., 2018; Venville et al., 2014; Thompson-Ebanks & Jarman, 2018).

##  Negotiation Burdens

Even after approval, students may need to explain accommodations repeatedly to instructors, challenge resistance, or monitor whether supports are actually implemented. This turns access into ongoing labor rather than an assured institutional process (Karpicz, 2020).

# 3. Why Reform Is Needed Now

## Rising Student Need

Postsecondary institutions are seeing increased demand related to mental health, neurodivergence, chronic illness, episodic disability, trauma, caregiving pressures, and financial stress. Many students experience functional barriers regardless of whether they possess formal documentation.

## Equity Commitments Require Structural Change

Universities cannot credibly claim commitment to equity while maintaining systems that advantage students with money, time, medical access, and bureaucratic confidence. Where access depends on privilege, equity goals remain unmet.

## Outdated Medicalized Assumptions

Traditional systems often rely on a deficit model: disability is treated as an individual medical problem requiring expert verification. Contemporary disability scholarship and self-advocacy instead emphasizes the role of institutions, environments, norms, and policies in producing barriers to access (Hamraie, 2017; Sins Invalid, 2019).

## Growing Peer Institution Innovation

Multiple universities have already adopted more flexible approaches, demonstrating that reform is practical and administratively feasible.

# 4. Policy Problem Statement

The core policy problem is not simply insufficient accommodations. It is that many accommodation systems are organized around gatekeeping rather than access.

This produces five recurring harms:

1.     **Delayed support** due to documentation and intake timelines

2.     **Unequal access** based on class, race, immigration status, and cultural capital

3.     **Underutilization** because students avoid stigmatizing processes

4.     **Faculty inconsistency** in implementation

5.     **Institutional reputational risk** where equity rhetoric is contradicted by student experience

# 5. Theory of Change

If institutions reduce unnecessary documentation barriers, shift responsibility for access from individual students to campus systems, and build transparent equitable processes, then more students will access support earlier, retention and trust will improve, and campuses will better align practice with public commitments.

This Advocacy Paper therefore advances a shift:
**From:** medically proving disability to receive retrofitted academic accommodations
**To:** designing equitable systems that remove barriers proactively and center access

The following sections review evidence, comparative models, policy options, and recommendations for implementation.

This Advocacy Paper draws on three complementary evidence sources:

A targeted review was conducted of postsecondary institutions that have adopted less medicalized accommodation practices or alternative documentation standards. Examples include Portland State University, University of Arizona, Syracuse University, Cornell University, Ohio State University, UCLA, and University of Guelph-Humber. These cases were examined to identify reform pathways, administrative models, and implementation risks.

This paper synthesizes interdisciplinary scholarship from disability studies, higher education studies, education policy, critical race theory, DisCrit (Disability Critical Race Studies), and Disability Justice. The review includes qualitative studies, systematic reviews, policy analyses, autoethnographies, and doctoral dissertations examining barriers to accommodations, disclosure dynamics, class inequality, racialized exclusion, and institutional reform.

## Source 3. Critical Policy Analysis

Beyond describing barriers, this Advocacy Paper evaluates how accommodation systems distribute burden, power, and legitimacy. It asks not only whether a system exists, but who benefits from its design, who is filtered out, and what assumptions are embedded in its procedures.

# 7. Literature Review: What Research Shows

## 7.1 Students Face Significant Barriers Accessing Accommodations

A substantial body of research demonstrates that many disabled students either do not access accommodation services or use them only selectively, despite experiencing significant barriers in their education. Research by M. et al. (2010) identified several factors that discourage students from seeking accommodations, including concerns about disability identity, fear of negative social reactions, limited awareness of available services, perceptions that supports are ineffective, and negative experiences with faculty. Similarly, Lyman et al. (2016) found that students often avoided accommodations because of stigma, feelings of not being “disabled enough,” concerns about fairness, burdensome processes, and unsatisfactory interactions with disability service offices. A systematic review by Lindsay et al. (2018) reported consistent barriers across studies, including stigma and discrimination, uncertainty about how to access support, instructor attitudes, course-specific factors, the nature of students’ disabilities, and the timing and risks associated with disclosure. Collectively, this research suggests that low rates of accommodation use should not be interpreted as evidence of low need; rather, they often reflect the inaccessibility, complexity, and social costs embedded within accommodation systems themselves.

## 7.2 Disclosure Is Complex, Strategic, and Often Risky

Traditional accommodation systems are premised on the assumption that students will disclose disability when they require support. However, research consistently demonstrates that disclosure is a complex and often fraught process. Venville et al. (2014) found that students with mental health disabilities engaged in ongoing assessments of the potential benefits and risks of disclosure, while Vergunst and Swartz (2021) similarly reported that graduate students with psychosocial disabilities carefully weighed concerns about stigma, misunderstanding, and future repercussions. Likewise, Thompson-Ebanks and Jarman (2018) found that students with nonapparent disabilities based disclosure decisions on factors such as trust, anticipated responses, and prior experiences. These findings suggest that disclosure is not a neutral administrative step but a decision shaped by unequal power relations and the potential consequences of being identified as disabled.

## 7.3 Current Systems Reproduce Class Inequality

Documentation-based accommodation systems often reproduce socioeconomic inequities by making access contingent on resources that are unevenly distributed. Research by Waterfield and Whelan (2017) at Dalhousie University found that students from higher socioeconomic backgrounds were more likely to obtain assessments, navigate institutional processes effectively, and access private supports such as tutoring when institutional accommodations were insufficient. In contrast, lower-income students faced financial barriers to obtaining documentation, had less familiarity with bureaucratic systems, reported greater concerns about stigma, and had fewer alternatives when supports failed. As a result, access to accommodations can depend as much on a student's available resources as on their actual support needs.

## 7.4 Race and Disability Are Deeply Interconnected

A growing body of scholarship argues that accommodation systems cannot be understood through disability alone, as experiences of access are shaped by intersecting forms of oppression. Stapleton and James (2020) contend that higher education disability research has frequently centered white experiences while overlooking racialized barriers, reproducing what they describe as “color-evasive” approaches to equity. Similarly, Baker (2019) found that Black disabled students in Ontario encountered intertwined forms of racism and ableism, including heightened scrutiny, demands for additional proof, and institutional skepticism. Gorman (2013) reported that white students with nonapparent disabilities were more likely to register with accessibility services than students of colour facing similar challenges, raising questions about who is recognized as deserving support. Drawing on the concept of racist ableism, Mireles (2022) further demonstrates how racialized students are often constructed as less capable, deviant, or academically inferior through racialized notions of ability. Together, these studies suggest that accommodation systems can reproduce racial inequities even when they operate through formally race-neutral policies.

## 7.5 Self-Advocacy Models Can Shift Institutional Burdens onto Students

Many postsecondary institutions frame self-advocacy as the primary pathway to access, requiring students to disclose, negotiate, and continually advocate for their needs. However, Karpicz (2020), in a study of disabled graduate students of colour, argues that this approach individualizes responsibility for access while obscuring the structural barriers that produce exclusion. Students who are unable or unwilling to self-advocate in institutionally sanctioned ways may be perceived as disengaged or lacking motivation, despite facing significant barriers. The study found that students often relied on alternative strategies, including selective non-disclosure, informal negotiation, refusal, and community-based forms of support outside official channels. These findings suggest that access should not depend on a student's confidence, available time, or capacity to repeatedly challenge institutional power.

# 14. Minimum Policy Direction

The literature reviewed in this white paper, together with comparative institutional examples and student-led reform initiatives, suggests that maintaining strictly documentation-driven accommodation systems is increasingly difficult to justify. Research consistently demonstrates that documentation requirements can delay support, reproduce socioeconomic and racial inequities, discourage help-seeking, and place disproportionate burdens on students. At the same time, examples from postsecondary institutions across North America demonstrate that more flexible approaches are both possible and already in use.

The evidence suggests that **Option 3: Access-Centered Reform** should be understood as the minimum standard of reform that institutions ought to pursue. This approach reduces reliance on medical documentation while maintaining clear processes for review, consistency, and accountability. Core elements include low-barrier intake processes, temporary accommodations while documentation is being gathered, proportional evidence standards, recognition of self-report and functional impacts, transparent decision-making criteria, accessible appeals processes, shared responsibility for access across institutional actors, and expanded use of universal design principles.

This should not be understood as a radical or aspirational model. Rather, Option 3 represents a pragmatic baseline that responds directly to the barriers identified in the literature while building on practices that have already been implemented at other institutions. **Option 4: Fully De-Medicalized Access-Centered Model** remains an important long-term horizon for institutions committed to transforming access and reducing reliance on individualized accommodation systems. However, the evidence reviewed here suggests that institutions need not wait for system-wide transformation before addressing the most significant barriers embedded within current accommodation processes. At a minimum, institutions should adopt the reforms outlined in Option 3.

# 16. Policy Recommendations

The evidence suggests that meaningful reform requires more than administrative efficiency. Institutions must redesign accommodation systems around access, equity, transparency, and shared responsibility. The following recommendations are structured for practical implementation.

## Recommendation 1: Reduce Reliance on Medical Documentation

### Action

Adopt a proportional evidence model in which students may establish need through multiple pathways, including:

·      student self-attestation

·      prior accommodation history

·      functional impact statements

·      existing records already on file

·      professional documentation where absolutely necessary/as a last resort

### Rationale

Heavy documentation requirements disproportionately burden low-income students, racialized students, international students, and students without consistent healthcare access (Waterfield & Whelan, 2017; Shanouda & Spagnuolo, 2021).

### Implementation Steps

1.     Revise documentation policy language.

2.     Eliminate repeated proof for longterm disabilities and chronic illnesses.

3.     Create functional-needs as opposed to functional limitations intake forms.

4.     Define limited circumstances requiring specialist documentation.

## Recommendation 2: Introduce Immediate Temporary Supports

### Action

Allow students to receive interim accommodations while review is underway.

Examples:

·      note-taking supports

·      short-term flexibility

·      testing adjustments

·      attendance flexibility where reasonable

### Rationale

Students should not lose academic standing while waiting weeks or months for paperwork.

### Implementation Steps

1.     Establish automatic 30–60 day interim supports.

2.     Create emergency intake pathway.

3.     Fast-track urgent cases mid-semester.

## Recommendation 3: Build Transparent Appeals and Conflict Resolution

### Action

Create formal review and appeal processes when accommodations are denied, delayed, or poorly implemented.

### Rationale

Many current systems depend on informal negotiation where power favors institutions.

### Implementation Steps

1.     Publish appeal timelines.

2.     Create independent review panel.

3.     Permit student advocates/support persons.

4.     Track recurring dispute themes.

## Recommendation 4: Shift Faculty Responsibility Toward Inclusive Course Design

### Action

Move some access supports upstream through accessible pedagogy and course design.

Examples:

·      flexible assessment formats

·      captioned media

·      accessible documents

·      reasonable deadline flexibility frameworks

·      multiple participation pathways

### Rationale

Not every barrier should require an individual accommodation letter.

### Implementation Steps

1.     Faculty training modules.

2.     Department accessibility infrastructure.

3.     Incentives in teaching evaluations and grants.

4.     Include access reviews in curriculum approvals.

## Recommendation 5: Embed Intersectional Equity Review
### Action
Audit accommodation systems for disparities linked to race, class, gender, migration status, and disability type.
### Rationale
Race-neutral systems can still produce unequal outcomes (Stapleton & James, 2020; Baker, 2019).
### Implementation Steps

1.     Collect voluntary demographic data.

2.     Review approval rates, delays, appeals, withdrawals.

3.     Conduct student climate surveys.

4.     Publish annual equity reports.

## Recommendation 6: Create Student Co-Governance Structures

### Action

Include disabled students in governance, policy design, and evaluation.

### Rationale

Students possess lived expertise often absent from administrative design.

### Implementation Steps

1.     Establish Accessibility Advisory Council.

2.     Compensate student members for their labor and expertise.

3.     Include graduate, undergraduate, international, and multiply marginalized students.

4.     Require consultation on major policy changes.

## Recommendation 7: Advocate for Provincial/Funder Reform

### Action

Work with governments and sector bodies to revise funding formulas and accessibility rules that incentivize restrictive documentation practices.

### Rationale

Some institutional gatekeeping is driven by external compliance and funding structures.

### Implementation Steps

1.     Joint submissions through university associations.

2.     Provincial roundtables with students and institutions.

3.     Recommend broader eligibility criteria.

# 18. What Not to Do

Reform efforts often fail when institutions:

·      rename offices without changing rules

·      increase discretion without accountability

·      overburden faculty without support

·      consult students symbolically only

·      treat access as a niche service issue rather than campus governance issue

# 19. Conclusion

Postsecondary institutions increasingly position themselves as leaders in equity, inclusion, and accessibility. Yet many accommodation systems continue to rely on documentation-heavy models that require students to prove disability, disclose personal information, and navigate complex administrative processes before receiving support that is often inadequate. The evidence reviewed in this Advocacy Paper suggests that these systems can reproduce class inequality, racial inequity, and disability stigma while delaying access to necessary supports.

At the same time, the literature, comparative institutional examples, and student-led reform efforts demonstrate that alternatives already exist. More flexible approaches to documentation, intake, review, and support provision have been implemented across North American institutions, showing that reform is both feasible and achievable. Collectively, this evidence supports **Option 3: Access-Centered Reform** as a minimum standard for institutional action. By reducing unnecessary medical gatekeeping while maintaining transparent and accountable processes, institutions can improve access, strengthen student trust, support retention and success, and better align accommodation systems with their stated equity commitments.

The central question is no longer whether reform is possible, but whether institutions are willing to shift responsibility for access from individual students to the systems that shape educational participation.

# 20. Limitations and Future Research

This Advocacy Paper draws on published research, publicly available institutional policies, and documented student advocacy initiatives to identify barriers within accommodation systems and examine emerging reform models. While the environmental scan identifies a range of promising practices, it also reveals important gaps in the available evidence. In particular, limited information exists regarding how institutions developed, approved, implemented, and evaluated the policy changes described in this report. Publicly available policies rarely document the governance processes, stakeholder consultations, resource investments, organizational changes, or implementation challenges that shaped reform efforts. As a result, while this paper is able to identify alternative approaches and potential policy directions, it is less able to assess how reforms were achieved or how effectively they function in practice over time.

A similar limitation exists with respect to student-led advocacy initiatives and faculty interventions. Although many campaigns and efforts have articulated clear demands and desires for reduced documentation requirements, greater reliance on student self-report, and more accessible accommodation systems, comparatively little information is available about the long-term outcomes of these efforts, the institutional responses they generated, or the conditions under which advocacy successfully influenced policy change.

These limitations point to several priorities for future research. There is a need for detailed case studies of institutions that have adopted equity-centered accommodation reforms, including examinations of how policy changes were approved, which stakeholders were involved, what barriers emerged during implementation, and how success was measured. Research should also investigate the outcomes of student-led accessibility campaigns and facculty-led interventions, including the strategies used, the factors associated with successful reform, and the role of students and faculty in institutional governance and policy development. Finally, longitudinal studies examining the effects of accommodation reform on student access, retention, academic success, satisfaction, and institutional trust would help strengthen the evidence base for future policy change across the postsecondary sector.
